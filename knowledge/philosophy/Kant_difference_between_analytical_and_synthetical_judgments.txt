# TITLE: OF THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN ANALYTICAL AND SYNTHETICAL JUDGMENTS

-> Kant distinguishes between two types of judgments: analytical and synthetical

    -> Analytical Judgment = statements where the predicate is already contained in the subject
        -> True by definition and do not add new information
            -> Ex. "All bachelors are unmarried." where the concept of "unmarried" is already included in "bachelor".
        * These are a priori and dot need/require experience

    -> Syntehtical Judgments = these add new information to the subject, expanding our knowledge
        -> Ex. "The suhn will rise tomorrow." where the concept of "rising tomorrow" is not contained in "the sun".
        * These judgments can be a posterori (based on experience) or a priori (what Kant is most interested in--as am I)

    -> Kant's Big Question: Are there synthetical judgments that are a priori?
        -> If so, they would provide new knowledge without relying on experience.
            -> This is the foundation of metaphysics and pure mathematics.
        -> Kant argues that disciplines like mathematics and certain principles of physics rely on synthetical a priori judgments.
    
    -> This distinction is crucial to his Critique of Pure Reason.
        -> It sets up his argument that knowledge isn't just about definitions (analytic) or experience (synthetic a posterori).
            -> It's about how reason itself can expand knowledge through synthetic a priori judgments.
